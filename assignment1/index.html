A1 - Interactive Experience Research Project

Q1. What was the first thing you paid attention to when interacting with the experience?

The first thing that caught my attention was the dramatic symphonic background music, which instantly created an immersive mood. At the same time, the large opening title moved with the rhythm of the music, giving me a sense of performance. In addition, I noticed the “scroll for story” instruction, and as I scrolled, the building was revealed from different perspectives. It felt as if I was entering a real hall and exploring Gehry's architecture step by step.

Q2. Spend two minutes with the experience and create a list of each of your discrete actions.

During the first two minutes, I engaged in several actions that defined the interaction. I listened to the orchestral music, which set the mood, and then observed the animated title moving in rhythm. Following the description, I scrolled down to view the hall from multiple perspectives. I clicked on navigation elements to access different sections and read brief texts that explained Gehry’s process. Finally, I watched video clips embedded on the website. These actions combined sound, text, and visuals to form a layered, narrative experience.

Q3. What part of the experience did you spend the most time engaging with?

The part I spent the most time on was the scrolling feature, which acted as this website’s main navigation tool. Through scrolling, I accessed perspectives of the hall, short videos, articles, and archival images. The design encouraged continuous exploration, creating a flow similar to moving through an exhibition. I found this section engaging because it combined multiple media formats in a seamless structure. The balance of sound, images, and narrative text made scrolling an immersive experience rather than a simple mechanical action.

Q4. What was the most common action in your two minute interaction with the experience?

The most common action was scrolling, which served as the main navigation method. Each scroll revealed new layers of content, including images, text and videos, presented in a continuous sequence. Unlike other websites that rely heavily on menus or clicks, this design used scrolling as a narrative device, guiding users naturally through the story. The repetitive action also reinforced immersion, echoing the rhythm of the orchestral soundtrack. This made scrolling not only functional but also part of the aesthetic experience, turning a simple action into immersive and meaningful engagement.

Q5. What is your impression of the intended primary goal of the interactive experience?

The intended goal is to present Frank Gehry’s creative process and the cultural significance of the Walt Disney Concert Hall in a way that is both accessible and memorable. The website is not only like a static archive, but also tells a story through music, text, images and video. By blending these elements, it communicates architectural value and aesthetic innovation in a narrative style. The design aims to inspire curiosity and deepen appreciation for the cultural and architectural value of Gehry’s work.

Q6. How does the interactive experience communicate this primary goal?

The website communicates this goal through its multimedia design. The orchestral soundtrack sets the mood and atmosphere, while the continuous scrolling reveals visuals, images, and text in designed layers. Short videos give more information, allowing users to understand Gehry’s creative thinking directly. This design simulates walking through an exhibition, which reinforces Gehry’s architectural vision. By combining sound, imagery and text, the experience makes Gehry’s concept accessible to a wide audience and ensures that the intended message is communicated clearly and effectively.

Q7. What is your impression of how the experience should be interacted with over time?

My impression is that the experience is designed for repeated interaction rather than a single visit. In one session, a visitor can explore the main narrative and gain an overview of Gehry’s design process. However, the depth of content, including collections, videos, and detailed information, encourages further visits. Each return allows users to interact with new aspects they may have missed previously. This design reflects the way people often revisit cultural spaces like museums, suggesting that the website intends to provide both short-term and long-term value.

Q8. How does the interactive experience communicate how it should be interacted with over time?

The continuous scrolling and distinct sections, including articles, images, and videos, make it clear that there is more to explore than one session allows. Each section feels like a chapter in a larger narrative, encouraging users to return and continue. The presence of collections and varied media also signals that repeated exploration is valuable. This structure suggests that the website is designed for sustained engagement over time.

Q9. What other media forms (digital or otherwise) does the experience reference?

The website uses several other media forms to shape its identity. Its sequential flow resembles a curated museum exhibition, where each section is presented to build a story. The photographs or images reference traditional photography as a method of documenting cultural events. Short documentary-style videos further connect the website to film traditions. Finally, the orchestral soundtrack references live performance, directly linking the design to the hall’s function as a concert place.

Q10. What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should act when engaging with your research experience?

It communicates that I should behave as if I am visiting an exhibition or cultural performance. On this website, I am encouraged to take time, move carefully through the content, and focus on individual details. The design signals that each section deserves attention and that the experience works best when approached thoughtfully. In this way, the website encourages users to interact with each element like an audience member in a gallery or museum.

Q11. What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should feel when engaging with your research experience?

The reference communicates that I should feel a sense of admiration and cultural respect. The orchestral soundtrack creates emotional intensity, while the imagery and videos evoke the feeling associated with architecture. Together, they create an atmosphere of wonder, encouraging people to appreciate this website not only as information but also as an artistic performance. This helps shape my interaction, guiding me to feel inspired, engaged and appreciative of the creativity behind both the architecture itself and the website’s design.

Q12. What is the most frustrating part of the interaction to you and what makes that part frustrating?

The most frustrating part is the heavy reliance on continuous scrolling. While it creates immersion, it can also become tiring and inefficient, especially when trying to locate a specific section quickly. Unlike traditional navigation bars that allow direct access, scrolling requires moving through all content sequentially. This repetition reduces usability during multiple visits, when efficiency becomes more important than immersion. Although the design creates a narrative flow, it sacrifices practicality, which can cause frustration for users.

Q13. What is the most satisfying part of the interaction to you and what makes that part satisfying?

The most satisfying part of the interaction is the harmony between the orchestral soundtrack and the layered visual design. As I scrolled, the music created a rhythm that matched the unfolding images and text, producing a very strong emotional impact. This alignment of sound and visuals gave me the sensation of being inside the hall, experiencing both performance and architecture at the same time. The coherence between different media forms made the interaction memorable and engaging.
